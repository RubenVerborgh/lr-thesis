Abstract

Over the past three decades, the Web, as a communication medium, has become increasingly an essential environment for conducting various aspects of scholarly communication. The effects of the Web alongside societal conditions has situated the global scholarship system in a constant flux of change. The contemporary scholarly communication paradigm operates under a diverse set of sociotechnical constraints, with observable inefficiencies stressing its ecosystem, and the emergence of anomalies due to dominant economic forces and the adoption of non-interoperable system designs. Through a communal process, units of information are continuously created, registered, and exchanged on the Web. Researchers and academics are immersed with online scholarly activities en route to disseminating their findings, exchanging ideas, and collaborating over new investigations. However, humans and machines have restricted access with constrained reuse, researchers and the general public are confronted with high costs to produce, to share, and to read, as well as hindered to efficiently discover and build useful connections in our collective knowledge. Furthermore, the digital identities in the ecosystem and contributions to the - so called - scholarly commons are shaped, owned, or controlled by (for- and non-profit) third-party entities, as well as individuals being required to use particular systems while being exposed to security and privacy implications. In order to have a frame of reference for the thesis and to approach improving the efficiency and democratic functioning of research and scientific communication, the adoption of technological communication mediums alongside scientific paradigms in which scholarship has historically operated is examined. The thesis reviews the prior work to identify gaps in knowledge and technical standards in context of the Web for common use cases, including: the core forces and functions in scientific communication and their interplay; the structure and semantics of scholarly information; the efforts to decentralise and interoperable mechanisms to publish, disseminate, and interact with scholarly artefacts. First, the research findings posit the Linked Data Notifications (LDN) as a communication protocol that describes how independently built applications can perform the roles of senders, receivers, and consumers by facilitating the design of loosely coupled systems that can exchange structured notifications as Linked Data. LDN's underlying mechanism triggers registration, fulfills awareness, and supports the certification and archiving functions in scientific communication in a decentralised ecosystem. Second, dokieli is posited as a decentralised client application based on the Open Web Platform allowing autonomous actors to exchange units of communication with personal data services. dokieli demonstrates how an assembly of Web standards can come together in a cohesive way in order to enable actors to reach a high degree of control over their creations and social activities. Based on the findings, the article also induces a conceptual generalisation aligned with the architecture of the Web in enabling a new generation of a scholarly ecosystem. The overarching conclusion of the thesis is that it is possible to distribute scholarly contributions on the Web based on an actor (or contributor) centric model while still adhering to the core functional requirements of scholarly communication. This may necessitate a revisit the roles that individual actors can perform in the scholarly ecosystem. Decoupling of the forces and functions in context of the Web necessitates interoperable applications through the use of standard data formats and communication protocols, and consequently enabling users to switch between standards-compliant applications without interfering with existing data. With the adoption of a Web-centric communication flow requires the scholarly ecosystem to unchain itself from antiquated practices and inadequate use of the available medium. The social conventions that surrounds the notion of a "scholarly contribution" should be revisited in order to improve the accessibility of knowledge, and be more inclusive and equitable for everyone.
